Instead, like our adversaries, we shall assert that the struggle between good and evil is simply a struggle about the role of man in the universe. Is man "free" or is he property?

Thus evil, if such a term is needed, is simply, for us, the initiation of violence.

Every modern government makes a "social contract"—a pact—without, of course, the consent of the populace—that it will protect and care for its citizens if they give up their right to care for themselves.

Prometheus is the divine rebel—a "Satan" who commits god-like crimes against the patriarchy. This is the critical point: that Prometheus, like Satan, is not man's enemy but is the enemy of the authoritarian tyrant whose desire is to rule man.

Disobedience, as well as pride (independence), are the two fundamental sins of the "evil-one." In fact, sin in the religious as well as the secular context is simply disobedience—the refusal to be a slave.

There is a significant difference between praying and trading. During begging, impossible promises are made. In trading there is an exchange of something tangible for something tangible. The person wants something and the spirit wants something. They make a deal.

To make a pact with the Devil or any spirit is to negotiate for yourself. This is true evil—according to the church and society. You have rejected their help, their intermediaries, and most importantly, their authority.

Lucifer is more the Greek Prometheus than the Christian Satan. Thus, a pact with Lucifer-now-Satan—he who is loathed by the keepers and the shepherds and their slaves—is man contracting with a friend: Prometheus-Lucifer-Satan.

## PART I: HISTORY

### Chapter One: The Foundations

The book that you are holding in your hands represents a corpus of literature that forms the very heart of the European esoteric tradition.

In the last twenty-five years or so, books on "magic" have concentrated largely on Kabbalism, Jungian psychology or the "neo-paganism" of so-called "Wicca." But for the three centuries following the Renaissance, the serious practice of magical art consisted essentially of the kind of systems collected in this book.

This material represents what is normally considered "black magic," since the spirits it pretends to consort with are admittedly demons. It is also, as we intend to show, transcendental magic, since the practitioner places his very soul and mind on the line in order to become more than a man: To achieve the power of a Magus.

The three Grimoires collected and adapted in Part II—The Grimorium Verum, The Grand Grimoire and The Constitution of Honorius—first saw some kind of general distribution between the end of the seventeenth century in France, during the twilight of the reign of Louis XIV, and the middle of the century following.

**The Aristocracy of Black Magic**

In spite of its status as a Catholic country, France was the spot in Europe that proved the most fertile and safe ground for the professional magicians. These people escaped the witch burnings of the period for two reasons: first, the principle victims of the witch trials were the peasantry of Lutheran and Calvinist countries. Contrary to historical myth, the Inquisition was far less destructive of human life than were the new Protestant sects. And second, the people who formed this underground were more or less educated people who were protected by their clients, the aristocracy.

**The Diabolical Duke**

At the top of the pyramid of these "predatory" personalities was Phillippe, Duc D'Orleans, nephew of the King and one of the people in line for the throne of France. He was brilliant, literate, amoral, literally murderous if crossed, an active bisexual and, since his youth, a practicing adept of the black arts.

### Chapter Two: Possible Origins

**The Hellish Priesthood**

The French priesthood at this time contained an enormous class of impoverished and mostly out-of-work priests and "abbes" that were far too numerous to find a real place in the functioning hierarchy of the Church. They supplemented their meager incomes and fended off starvation by selling their services — seduction, the gaining of wealth, and even murder.

The mass was viewed as a magical act that had power in and of itself regardless of the purpose for which it was performed.

**Dark Gods of Africa**

During the period when the Grimoires first appeared, the colony of Haiti, then called San Domingue, was the richest possession of France. It seems almost certain that a number of Europeans took an interest in, and learned from, the imported beliefs that later became Voodoo. The three rituals we have here all begin with extended purification and sacrifice, as well as the ritual creation of the proper tools — exactly paralleling Afro-Caribbean magical processes.

**The Affair of the Poisons**

La Voisin was the organizer of a Mafia-like group of sorcerers, alchemists and renegade priests who had garnered a fortune by performing black magic and compounding poisons for wealthy clients. The Marquise De Montespan, mistress of Louis XIV, was deeply implicated — engaging in black masses to maintain the King's favor, including ceremonies at which a small child's throat was cut over the chalice as offering.

**Hellfire Dashwood**

Sir Francis Dashwood founded the Order of the Friars of Medmenham, celebrating in an abandoned Cistercian monastery on a mist-shrouded island in the Thames. Members included the Earl of Sandwich and Benjamin Franklin. Inscribed over the abbey entrance: Rabelais' dictum, "Do what you will."

### Chapter Four: The Origin of the Pact

The demonic pact was and remains one of the most anxiety-ridden subjects in the western esoteric tradition. The conjuration of a demon and the creation of a formal alliance with it is quite possibly the oldest form of initiation known to man.

**Give-Man**

In Haitian Voodoo, there is a ceremony called the Ba Moun or "give-man" ceremony. The person making the agreement can give other people's souls — specifically from their own family and friends. One person a year will mysteriously sicken and die until the bargainer himself is taken.

**Archaic Roots**

The pact-working was cut off from its ultimate roots in prehistoric magic to become the dangerous selling of one's soul to everlasting hellfire. The Theophilus legend contains all the ingredients: the scholar driven to extremis who uses forbidden knowledge to call up an evil spirit, enjoys magical power, suffers last-minute regret, and faces Hell.

### Chapter Five: Pacts in the Church

**Popes of Satan**

Gerbert (Pope Sylvester II, 11th century) supposedly made a pact with Lucifer to become Pope, with the condition he must never say mass in Jerusalem. He was tricked — the church where he finally said mass turned out to be called "the Church of Jerusalem."

Roderigo Borgia, Pope Alexander VI: father to Cesare and Lucrezia, patron of hermetic texts, suspected of incestuous relationships with both children, responsible for ordering one of the major European witch hunts. During his tenure the Corpus Hermeticum was translated into Latin for the first time.

### Chapter Six: The Legacy of Faust

There were two historical "Faustus" figures: one a legitimate scholar of radical ideas, the other a charlatan who "lied and buggered his way across Germany." Most popular stories are based on the latter.

Marlowe's Doctor Faustus sends him to Hell. Goethe's version — written as a gambler's bet rather than an act of fatalism — ultimately saves him. The myth of Faustus became a symbol of opposition against the sterile and materialistic direction of post-Reformation society.

Not only did Dionysus and Prometheus contribute to the myth of the Devil, but they were equally major contributors to the Jesus myth as well. Both gods came to Earth, brought new knowledge, and both "died" for the effort — one (Dionysus) was resurrected and ascended to heaven. **Demonology**

The fundamental definition of a demon, without theological baggage, is a non-human entity of great intelligence and power, existing as mind and spirit, and capable of influencing the material world.

In ancient times all demons — good and bad — were considered as a single class. Christianity created the myth of the soul-stealing demon to quash magical practice outside the Church.

In Haiti, the Loa are not quite omniscient or omnipotent — they need things from us and as a result, deals are struck. The Voudouist believes what he experiences. Such a person believes in spirits because he has spoken to one and been spoken to in return.
